Chapter 1: Introduction to Peripheral Intervention Hardware

Modern peripheral arterial intervention is fundamentally a hardware-driven specialty. Unlike many areas of medicine where outcomes depend predominantly on diagnosis or pharmacology, success in peripheral intervention is often determined by the operator's understanding of devices. Many trainees assume that the most important decision in a peripheral intervention is choosing the stent. In reality, experienced operators recognize that stent selection represents only a small part of the decision tree. Most failures occur much earlier. A procedure fails because:

  • Access was suboptimal.
  • Support was inadequate.
  • The lesion could not be crossed.
  • The wrong wire was chosen.
  • Calcium modification was insufficient.

The modern philosophy of peripheral intervention can therefore be summarized as:

Access → Support → Cross → Prepare → Scaffold

Every device used in peripheral intervention serves one of these purposes. The interventionist must understand not only the indications of each device but also the engineering principles that determine how these devices behave within diseased vessels. Peripheral arterial disease presents unique challenges compared with coronary or neurovascular disease. Lesions are often:

  • Long.
  • Diffuse.
  • Heavily calcified.
  • Chronic total occlusions.
  • Located in vessels exposed to flexion and compression.

Because of these challenges, peripheral interventions require a broader range of devices than most other endovascular specialties. The objective of this chapter series is not merely to list available devices but to explain why specific hardware is selected in specific clinical situations.

Chapter 2: Access Hardware and Sheaths

Why Access Matters

Access determines procedural success before the first wire even enters the lesion. A poorly chosen access site creates:

  • Insufficient support.
  • Excessive device length requirements.
  • Difficulty crossing lesions.
  • Increased complication rates.

An experienced operator often spends more time planning access than selecting a stent.

Micropuncture Sets

Modern peripheral interventions almost universally begin with a micropuncture set. The micropuncture technique minimizes vessel trauma while providing safe vascular entry. Components include:

  • 21G needle
  • 0.018 inch guidewire
  • Transition dilator
  • Small introducer sheath

Advantages include reduced bleeding complications, reduced risk of posterior wall puncture and easier ultrasound-guided access.

Sheath Physics

A sheath is not merely a conduit. It serves four critical functions:

  • Provides support.
  • Facilitates device exchange.
  • Permits contrast injection.
  • Maintains hemostasis.

Long sheaths significantly improve pushability and torque transmission. This becomes particularly important during femoropopliteal and tibial interventions.

Common Peripheral Sheaths

Sheath Primary Use Strength
Balkin Crossover Support
Ansel CTO work High support
Destination Tibials Flexibility
Flexor Long interventions Trackability

The choice of sheath should always be made before selecting wires and catheters because support requirements determine procedural strategy.

Chapter 3: Guidewire Science and Engineering

Guidewires are the most important tools in peripheral intervention. A guidewire is not simply a metal wire. It is a highly engineered device designed to balance:

  • Trackability
  • Torqueability
  • Pushability
  • Support
  • Penetration force
  • Safety

Understanding wire physics allows the operator to predict behavior during lesion crossing.

Core Construction

Most guidewires consist of:

  • Core wire
  • Spring coil
  • Polymer jacket
  • Hydrophilic coating

Different combinations of these elements create dramatically different clinical behavior.

Trackability

Trackability refers to the ability of the wire to follow vessel curves. A highly trackable wire easily navigates tortuous tibial arteries. Hydrophilic coatings improve trackability by reducing friction.

Torqueability

Torqueability describes how effectively rotational movement applied proximally is transmitted to the distal tip. Good torque response is essential during CTO interventions.

Pushability

Pushability determines how efficiently forward force is transmitted. Calcified lesions require greater pushability.

Support

Support becomes important after crossing. A wire that crosses easily may not provide sufficient support for device delivery. This explains why operators often exchange wires after successful lesion crossing.

Chapter 4: Crossing Wires

Crossing wires are designed primarily for navigation rather than support. Their goal is to safely find a path through the lesion. The majority of peripheral interventions begin with a crossing wire.

Fielder FC

The Fielder FC is among the most widely used peripheral crossing wires. Its popularity derives from its exceptional trackability and atraumatic tip design. The hydrophilic coating reduces friction and facilitates passage through tight stenoses. The wire is particularly useful in:

  • Tibial disease.
  • Long stenoses.
  • Soft CTOs.

A common mistake among trainees is escalating to aggressive wires too early. Experienced operators frequently achieve success with Fielder FC because they allow the wire to find the path of least resistance rather than forcing progression.

Command Wire

The Command wire functions as a workhorse platform. Compared with Fielder FC, it offers improved torque response while maintaining excellent flexibility. Applications include:

  • Routine femoropopliteal disease.
  • Soft CTOs.
  • Infrapopliteal interventions.

Many operators consider the Command wire their default first-line crossing wire.

Chapter 5: CTO Wires and Escalation Strategy

Chronic total occlusions represent one of the most challenging aspects of peripheral intervention. The greatest mistake made by inexperienced operators is choosing a highly penetrative wire too early. Successful CTO intervention requires gradual escalation.

Soft Wire → Intermediate Wire → CTO Wire → Retrograde Strategy

Gladius

The Gladius wire is designed for fibrotic CTOs. It offers greater penetration capability than traditional crossing wires while maintaining reasonable safety.

Halberd

Halberd occupies an intermediate position between workhorse wires and dedicated CTO wires. Its superior penetration capability allows traversal of resistant fibrotic caps.

Astato XS20

The Astato XS20 is a dedicated CTO penetration wire with approximately 20 grams tip load. It is intended for resistant proximal caps and calcified lesions. Unlike crossing wires, the Astato should be viewed as a penetration device. The goal is not navigation. The goal is controlled cap penetration.

Astato XS40

The Astato XS40 provides even greater penetration capability. Its use requires significant experience because the risk of vessel perforation increases substantially. The operator must continuously assess wire position using orthogonal fluoroscopic projections.

Winn Series

The Winn family represents some of the most aggressive CTO wires available. Examples include:

  • Winn 40
  • Winn 80
  • Winn 200T

These wires are reserved for heavily calcified chronic total occlusions. Because of their penetration power, they should never be used without adequate support catheter systems.

The Golden Rule of CTO Intervention

After successful crossing with an aggressive CTO wire:

Exchange the CTO wire for a workhorse wire before angioplasty.

This principle reduces perforation risk and improves device delivery. Failure to exchange wires is a common cause of procedural complications.

The art of CTO intervention lies not in selecting the strongest wire but in selecting the least aggressive wire capable of accomplishing the task. This philosophy distinguishes experienced interventionists from beginners.

Chapter 6: Support Catheters and Microcatheters

Many trainees believe guidewires determine procedural success. In reality, support catheters frequently determine whether a lesion can be crossed. A guidewire can only generate force if that force is transmitted efficiently. Without support, guidewire advancement often results in prolapse, buckling, or loss of position. Support catheters solve this problem.

Functions of Support Catheters

  • Improve pushability.
  • Improve torque transmission.
  • Facilitate wire exchange.
  • Permit selective vessel catheterization.
  • Improve CTO crossing success.

Support catheters essentially act as force amplifiers. They allow the operator to convert small hand movements into meaningful distal wire movement.

CXI Support Catheter

The CXI catheter is one of the most widely used support catheters in peripheral intervention. The device provides excellent support while maintaining flexibility. Common lengths include:

  • 65 cm
  • 90 cm
  • 135 cm
  • 150 cm

Applications include:

  • BTK interventions.
  • CTO crossing.
  • Wire exchange.
  • Selective tibial catheterization.

A common teaching pearl is:

If the wire keeps prolapsing, add support before escalating wire stiffness.

Quick-Cross Catheter

Quick-Cross catheters provide robust support while maintaining a relatively low profile. Advantages include:

  • Excellent wire exchange capability.
  • Good pushability.
  • Broad compatibility.

Navicross Catheter

The Navicross catheter is designed to facilitate vessel selection. The braided shaft improves torque transmission. The catheter is particularly useful in tortuous anatomy and tibial interventions.

Progreat Microcatheter

The Progreat occupies a unique position between support catheter and microcatheter. Its hydrophilic coating provides exceptional trackability. The catheter is particularly valuable in:

  • Tibial interventions.
  • Distal vessel selection.
  • Subintimal tracking.
  • Retrograde procedures.

One important feature of the Progreat system is the ability to intentionally form loops. Loop formation reduces the risk of perforation and facilitates controlled subintimal progression.

Chapter 7: CTO Crossing Algorithms

CTO intervention should never be random. Successful operators follow a structured escalation strategy. The objective is always to achieve lesion crossing using the least aggressive hardware necessary.

Soft CTO Algorithm

Fielder FC → Command → Support Catheter → Balloon

Soft occlusions frequently contain microchannels. Hydrophilic workhorse wires can often exploit these channels without requiring aggressive penetration. Patience is frequently more important than force.

Fibrotic CTO Algorithm

Command → Gladius → Halberd → CXI Support

Fibrotic lesions resist wire passage because of dense collagen deposition. These lesions require increased penetration capability while maintaining reasonable safety.

Calcified CTO Algorithm

Command → Halberd → Astato XS20 → Astato XS40

Calcified lesions often possess a heavily resistant proximal cap. The challenge is not lesion length but cap penetration. Failure to penetrate the proximal cap guarantees procedural failure.

Tibial CTO Algorithm

Below-the-knee disease presents unique challenges. The vessels are:

  • Small.
  • Tortuous.
  • Frequently calcified.

A typical tibial CTO strategy involves:

  • Fielder FC.
  • Command wire.
  • Progreat support.
  • Gladius escalation.
  • Retrograde access if required.

Retrograde Strategy

Retrograde access has revolutionized peripheral CTO treatment. When antegrade crossing fails, retrograde tibial or pedal access frequently succeeds. The distal cap is often softer than the proximal cap. This explains why retrograde approaches can dramatically increase procedural success rates.

Chapter 8: Balloon Technology

Balloon angioplasty remains the foundation of peripheral intervention. Although numerous advanced technologies exist, almost every procedure ultimately involves balloon inflation. Understanding balloon mechanics is therefore essential.

Objectives of Balloon Angioplasty

  • Plaque compression.
  • Plaque fracture.
  • Calcium disruption.
  • Lumen expansion.

Balloon Sizing Principles

Oversizing increases the risk of:

  • Dissection.
  • Perforation.
  • Elastic recoil.

Undersizing results in inadequate luminal gain. The general rule is:

Balloon Diameter ≈ Reference Vessel Diameter

Compliant Balloons

Compliant balloons expand as inflation pressure increases. Advantages include flexibility and conformability. Disadvantages include unpredictable diameter expansion.

Semi-Compliant Balloons

Semi-compliant balloons represent the most commonly used balloon type. They provide a balance between flexibility and predictable expansion.

Non-Compliant Balloons

Non-compliant balloons demonstrate minimal diameter change with increasing pressure. These devices are particularly useful in:

  • Fibrotic lesions.
  • In-stent restenosis.
  • Resistant stenoses.

High-Pressure Balloons

High-pressure balloons withstand nominal pressures in excess of 15 atmospheres. These devices are designed for calcified lesions and resistant stenoses.
