Chapter 1: Introduction to Peripheral Intervention Hardware

Modern peripheral arterial intervention is fundamentally a hardware-driven specialty. Unlike many areas of medicine where outcomes depend predominantly on diagnosis or pharmacology, success in peripheral intervention is often determined by the operator's understanding of devices. Many trainees assume that the most important decision in a peripheral intervention is choosing the stent. In reality, experienced operators recognize that stent selection represents only a small component of procedural success. Most failures occur much earlier. A procedure fails because:

  • Access was suboptimal.
  • Support was inadequate.
  • The lesion could not be crossed.
  • The wrong wire was chosen.
  • Calcium modification was insufficient.

The modern philosophy of peripheral intervention can therefore be summarized as:

Access → Support → Cross → Prepare → Scaffold

Every device used in peripheral intervention serves one of these purposes. The interventionist must understand not only the indications of each device but also the engineering principles that determine how these devices behave within diseased vessels. Peripheral arterial disease presents unique challenges compared with coronary or neurovascular disease. Lesions are often:

  • Long.
  • Diffuse.
  • Heavily calcified.
  • Chronic total occlusions.
  • Located in vessels exposed to flexion and compression.

Because of these challenges, peripheral interventions require a broader range of devices than most other endovascular specialties. The objective of this chapter series is not merely to list available devices but to explain why specific hardware is selected in specific clinical situations.

Chapter 2: Access Hardware and Sheaths

Why Access Matters

Access determines procedural success before the first wire even enters the lesion. A poorly chosen access site creates:

  • Insufficient support.
  • Excessive device length requirements.
  • Difficulty crossing lesions.
  • Increased complication rates.

An experienced operator often spends more time planning access than selecting a stent.

Micropuncture Sets

Modern peripheral interventions almost universally begin with a micropuncture set. The micropuncture technique minimizes vessel trauma while providing safe vascular entry. Components include:

  • 21G needle
  • 0.018 inch guidewire
  • Transition dilator
  • Small introducer sheath

Advantages include reduced bleeding complications, reduced risk of posterior wall puncture and easier ultrasound-guided access.

Sheath Physics

A sheath is not merely a conduit. It serves four critical functions:

  • Provides support.
  • Facilitates device exchange.
  • Permits contrast injection.
  • Maintains hemostasis.

Long sheaths significantly improve pushability and torque transmission. This becomes particularly important during femoropopliteal and tibial interventions.

Common Peripheral Sheaths

Sheath Primary Use Strength
Balkin Crossover Support
Ansel CTO work High support
Destination Tibials Flexibility
Flexor Long interventions Trackability

The choice of sheath should always be made before selecting wires and catheters because support requirements determine procedural strategy.

Chapter 3: Guidewire Science and Engineering

Guidewires are the most important tools in peripheral intervention. A guidewire is not simply a metal wire. It is a highly engineered device designed to balance:

  • Trackability
  • Torqueability
  • Pushability
  • Support
  • Penetration force
  • Safety

Understanding wire physics allows the operator to predict behavior during lesion crossing.

Core Construction

Most guidewires consist of:

  • Core wire
  • Spring coil
  • Polymer jacket
  • Hydrophilic coating

Different combinations of these elements create dramatically different clinical behavior.

Trackability

Trackability refers to the ability of the wire to follow vessel curves. A highly trackable wire easily navigates tortuous tibial arteries. Hydrophilic coatings improve trackability by reducing friction.

Torqueability

Torqueability describes how effectively rotational movement applied proximally is transmitted to the distal tip. Good torque response is essential during CTO interventions.

Pushability

Pushability determines how efficiently forward force is transmitted. Calcified lesions require greater pushability.

Support

Support becomes important after crossing. A wire that crosses easily may not provide sufficient support for device delivery. This explains why operators often exchange wires after successful lesion crossing.

Chapter 4: Crossing Wires

Crossing wires are designed primarily for navigation rather than support. Their goal is to safely find a path through the lesion. The majority of peripheral interventions begin with a crossing wire.

Fielder FC

The Fielder FC is among the most widely used peripheral crossing wires. Its popularity derives from its exceptional trackability and atraumatic tip design. The hydrophilic coating reduces friction and facilitates passage through tight stenoses. The wire is particularly useful in:

  • Tibial disease.
  • Long stenoses.
  • Soft CTOs.

A common mistake among trainees is escalating to aggressive wires too early. Experienced operators frequently achieve success with Fielder FC because they allow the wire to find the path of least resistance rather than forcing progression.

Command Wire

The Command wire functions as a workhorse platform. Compared with Fielder FC, it offers improved torque response while maintaining excellent flexibility. Applications include:

  • Routine femoropopliteal disease.
  • Soft CTOs.
  • Infrapopliteal interventions.

Many operators consider the Command wire their default first-line crossing wire.

Chapter 5: CTO Wires and Escalation Strategy

Chronic total occlusions represent one of the most challenging aspects of peripheral intervention. The greatest mistake made by inexperienced operators is choosing a highly penetrative wire too early. Successful CTO intervention requires gradual escalation.

Soft Wire → Intermediate Wire → CTO Wire → Retrograde Strategy

Gladius

The Gladius wire is designed for fibrotic CTOs. It offers greater penetration capability than traditional crossing wires while maintaining reasonable safety.

Halberd

Halberd occupies an intermediate position between workhorse wires and dedicated CTO wires. Its superior penetration capability allows traversal of resistant fibrotic caps.

Astato XS20

The Astato XS20 is a dedicated CTO penetration wire with approximately 20 grams tip load. It is intended for resistant proximal caps and calcified lesions. Unlike crossing wires, the Astato should be viewed as a penetration device. The goal is not navigation. The goal is controlled cap penetration.

Astato XS40

The Astato XS40 provides even greater penetration capability. Its use requires significant experience because the risk of vessel perforation increases substantially. The operator must continuously assess wire position using orthogonal fluoroscopic projections.

Winn Series

The Winn family represents some of the most aggressive CTO wires available. Examples include:

  • Winn 40
  • Winn 80
  • Winn 200T

These wires are reserved for heavily calcified chronic total occlusions. Because of their penetration power, they should never be used without adequate support catheter systems.

The Golden Rule of CTO Intervention

After successful crossing with an aggressive CTO wire:

Exchange the CTO wire for a workhorse wire before angioplasty.

This principle reduces perforation risk and improves device delivery. Failure to exchange wires is a common cause of procedural complications.

The art of CTO intervention lies not in selecting the strongest wire but in selecting the least aggressive wire capable of accomplishing the task. This philosophy distinguishes experienced interventionists from beginners.

Chapter 6: Support Catheters and Microcatheters

Many trainees believe guidewires determine procedural success. In reality, support catheters frequently determine whether a lesion can be crossed. A guidewire can only generate force if that force is transmitted efficiently. Without support, guidewire advancement often results in prolapse, buckling, or loss of position. Support catheters solve this problem.

Functions of Support Catheters

  • Improve pushability.
  • Improve torque transmission.
  • Facilitate wire exchange.
  • Permit selective vessel catheterization.
  • Improve CTO crossing success.

Support catheters essentially act as force amplifiers. They allow the operator to convert small hand movements into meaningful distal wire movement.

CXI Support Catheter

The CXI catheter is one of the most widely used support catheters in peripheral intervention. The device provides excellent support while maintaining flexibility. Common lengths include:

  • 65 cm
  • 90 cm
  • 135 cm
  • 150 cm

Applications include:

  • BTK interventions.
  • CTO crossing.
  • Wire exchange.
  • Selective tibial catheterization.

A common teaching pearl is:

If the wire keeps prolapsing, add support before escalating wire stiffness.

Quick-Cross Catheter

Quick-Cross catheters provide robust support while maintaining a relatively low profile. Advantages include:

  • Excellent wire exchange capability.
  • Good pushability.
  • Broad compatibility.

Navicross Catheter

The Navicross catheter is designed to facilitate vessel selection. The braided shaft improves torque transmission. The catheter is particularly useful in tortuous anatomy and tibial interventions.

Progreat Microcatheter

The Progreat occupies a unique position between support catheter and microcatheter. Its hydrophilic coating provides exceptional trackability. The catheter is particularly valuable in:

  • Tibial interventions.
  • Distal vessel selection.
  • Subintimal tracking.
  • Retrograde procedures.

One important feature of the Progreat system is the ability to intentionally form loops. Loop formation reduces the risk of perforation and facilitates controlled subintimal progression.

Chapter 7: CTO Crossing Algorithms

CTO intervention should never be random. Successful operators follow a structured escalation strategy. The objective is always to achieve lesion crossing using the least aggressive hardware necessary.

Soft CTO Algorithm

Fielder FC → Command → Support Catheter → Balloon

Soft occlusions frequently contain microchannels. Hydrophilic workhorse wires can often exploit these channels without requiring aggressive penetration. Patience is frequently more important than force.

Fibrotic CTO Algorithm

Command → Gladius → Halberd → CXI Support

Fibrotic lesions resist wire passage because of dense collagen deposition. These lesions require increased penetration capability while maintaining reasonable safety.

Calcified CTO Algorithm

Command → Halberd → Astato XS20 → Astato XS40

Calcified lesions often possess a heavily resistant proximal cap. The challenge is not lesion length but cap penetration. Failure to penetrate the proximal cap guarantees procedural failure.

Tibial CTO Algorithm

Below-the-knee disease presents unique challenges. The vessels are:

  • Small.
  • Tortuous.
  • Frequently calcified.

A typical tibial CTO strategy involves:

  • Fielder FC.
  • Command wire.
  • Progreat support.
  • Gladius escalation.
  • Retrograde access if required.

Retrograde Strategy

Retrograde access has revolutionized peripheral CTO treatment. When antegrade crossing fails, retrograde tibial or pedal access frequently succeeds. The distal cap is often softer than the proximal cap. This explains why retrograde approaches can dramatically increase procedural success rates.

Chapter 8: Balloon Technology

Balloon angioplasty remains the foundation of peripheral intervention. Although numerous advanced technologies exist, almost every procedure ultimately involves balloon inflation. Understanding balloon mechanics is therefore essential.

Objectives of Balloon Angioplasty

  • Plaque compression.
  • Plaque fracture.
  • Calcium disruption.
  • Lumen expansion.

Balloon Sizing Principles

Oversizing increases the risk of:

  • Dissection.
  • Perforation.
  • Elastic recoil.

Undersizing results in inadequate luminal gain. The general rule is:

Balloon Diameter ≈ Reference Vessel Diameter

Compliant Balloons

Compliant balloons expand as inflation pressure increases. Advantages include flexibility and conformability. Disadvantages include unpredictable diameter expansion.

Semi-Compliant Balloons

Semi-compliant balloons represent the most commonly used balloon type. They provide a balance between flexibility and predictable expansion.

Non-Compliant Balloons

Non-compliant balloons demonstrate minimal diameter change with increasing pressure. These devices are particularly useful in:

  • Fibrotic lesions.
  • In-stent restenosis.
  • Resistant stenoses.

High-Pressure Balloons

Certain lesions refuse to expand despite standard angioplasty. High-pressure balloons can generate extremely high inflation pressures. These devices are frequently used in heavily calcified lesions.

Chapter 9: Drug-Coated Balloons

Drug-coated balloons represent one of the most important advances in peripheral intervention. Traditional angioplasty addresses the mechanical component of stenosis but fails to address the biological response to vessel injury. Drug-coated balloons attempt to solve this problem.

Mechanism of Restenosis

After angioplasty:

  • Endothelial injury occurs.
  • Smooth muscle cells proliferate.
  • Neointimal hyperplasia develops.

This process ultimately causes restenosis. Drug-coated balloons deliver antiproliferative medication directly into the vessel wall.

Paclitaxel

Paclitaxel stabilizes microtubules and inhibits cellular proliferation. Advantages include:

  • Rapid uptake.
  • Prolonged tissue retention.
  • Potent antiproliferative effect.

Sirolimus

Sirolimus inhibits the mTOR pathway. The drug reduces smooth muscle proliferation while preserving endothelial healing. Many investigators believe sirolimus may represent the future of drug-coated technology.

Clinical Applications

  • SFA disease.
  • Popliteal disease.
  • In-stent restenosis.
  • Selected tibial interventions.

Advantages

  • No permanent implant.
  • Reduced restenosis.
  • Reduced target lesion revascularization.

Chapter 10: Calcium Modification Devices

Calcium is one of the greatest challenges in peripheral intervention. The presence of severe calcium increases:

  • Crossing difficulty.
  • Balloon resistance.
  • Stent under-expansion.
  • Restenosis.

Modern peripheral intervention increasingly emphasizes vessel preparation before definitive treatment.

Intravascular Lithotripsy (IVL)

IVL uses sonic pressure waves to fracture calcium. The technology is conceptually similar to extracorporeal shockwave lithotripsy used in urology. The balloon is inflated at low pressure. Energy pulses propagate through fluid and selectively fracture calcium.

Advantages of IVL

  • Minimal vessel trauma.
  • Low dissection rates.
  • Improved compliance.
  • Excellent safety profile.

Directional Atherectomy

Directional atherectomy physically removes plaque. Unlike angioplasty, which compresses plaque, atherectomy debulks the lesion.

TurboHawk System

TurboHawk utilizes a rotating cutter housed within a directional catheter. Plaque is shaved and collected within the catheter nosecone. Advantages include:

  • Plaque removal.
  • Reduced need for stenting.
  • Improved vessel preparation.

Orbital Atherectomy

Orbital atherectomy utilizes an eccentrically mounted crown. Rotation creates a sanding effect that preferentially modifies calcified tissue.

Rotational Atherectomy

Rotational systems employ high-speed rotating burrs. These devices fragment plaque into microscopic particles.

Future Direction

The future of peripheral intervention increasingly focuses on:

Lesion Preparation → Drug Delivery → Implant Avoidance

This strategy seeks to maximize luminal gain while minimizing the need for permanent implants.

Chapter 11: Atherectomy Systems

Atherectomy refers to endovascular plaque removal. Unlike conventional balloon angioplasty, which compresses plaque against the vessel wall, atherectomy physically removes or modifies plaque burden. The concept is particularly attractive in heavily calcified lesions where balloon angioplasty alone often produces inadequate lumen gain and significant dissections. The rationale behind atherectomy is simple:

Remove plaque first → Prepare vessel → Deliver definitive therapy

Why Atherectomy?

  • Reduces plaque burden.
  • Improves vessel compliance.
  • Facilitates drug delivery.
  • Reduces need for bailout stenting.
  • Improves luminal gain.

The modern concept of vessel preparation frequently combines atherectomy with drug-coated balloons.

Directional Atherectomy

Directional atherectomy devices physically shave plaque from one side of the vessel wall. The operator can orient the cutter toward the target plaque. This provides excellent plaque debulking while preserving uninvolved vessel segments.

TurboHawk

TurboHawk is one of the best-known directional atherectomy devices. The system consists of:

  • Directional cutter.
  • Collection chamber (nosecone).
  • Rotational drive mechanism.

During operation, plaque is shaved and stored within the nosecone. Multiple passes are usually required.

Advantages

  • Excellent plaque removal.
  • Useful in eccentric lesions.
  • Good vessel preparation.

Disadvantages

  • Distal embolization.
  • Longer procedure time.
  • Need for embolic protection.

Orbital Atherectomy

Orbital atherectomy employs an eccentrically mounted crown rotating at high speed. As rotational speed increases, the effective treatment diameter enlarges. The crown preferentially sands calcium while preserving softer tissue.

Advantages

  • Excellent calcium modification.
  • Useful in long calcified lesions.
  • Improves vessel compliance.

Rotational Atherectomy

Rotational systems utilize a high-speed rotating burr. These devices fragment plaque into microscopic particles. The concept resembles coronary rotational atherectomy but is adapted for peripheral anatomy.

Clinical Applications

  • Calcified SFA disease.
  • Calcified CFA disease.
  • In-stent restenosis.
  • Popliteal disease.

Atherectomy should not be viewed as a standalone treatment. The greatest benefits occur when combined with contemporary vessel preparation strategies and drug delivery technologies.

Chapter 12: Peripheral Stents

Despite advances in vessel preparation and drug-coated technology, stents remain a cornerstone of peripheral intervention. A stent acts as a permanent scaffold preventing elastic recoil and maintaining luminal patency. However, not all peripheral stents are the same. The biomechanical environment of the superficial femoral and popliteal arteries is fundamentally different from coronary circulation. Peripheral stents must tolerate:

  • Compression.
  • Torsion.
  • Elongation.
  • Flexion.
  • External trauma.

Balloon Expandable Stents

These stents are mounted on angioplasty balloons and expand when the balloon inflates. Advantages:

  • Precise deployment.
  • High radial force.
  • Excellent ostial accuracy.

Typical indications include:

  • Iliac lesions.
  • Renal artery stenosis.
  • Ostial disease.

Self Expanding Stents

Self-expanding nitinol stents represent the dominant stent platform in peripheral intervention. Nitinol possesses shape-memory and superelastic properties. After deployment, the stent gradually expands to its predetermined diameter.

Advantages

  • Flexibility.
  • Fatigue resistance.
  • Fracture resistance.
  • Excellent conformability.

Examples

  • Zilver Flex.
  • Absolute Pro.
  • Misago.
  • Innova.

Open Cell vs Closed Cell

Open-cell stents provide greater flexibility. Closed-cell stents provide superior scaffolding. The choice depends upon lesion morphology and vessel biomechanics.

Chapter 13: Supera Stent Masterclass

Among all peripheral stents, none has generated greater interest than the Supera stent. For fellowship examinations, board examinations and viva discussions, Supera deserves special attention.

Why Is Supera Different?

Most peripheral nitinol stents are laser-cut tubes. Supera is fundamentally different. It is an interwoven nitinol stent. Rather than being cut from a tube, multiple nitinol strands are braided together. The resulting architecture resembles a Chinese finger trap. This design provides extraordinary flexibility while maintaining high radial strength.

Biomechanical Advantages

  • Compression resistance.
  • Torsional resistance.
  • Flexion resistance.
  • Crush resistance.
  • Low fracture rates.

Why Popliteal Artery Is Challenging

The popliteal artery undergoes repeated:

  • Knee flexion.
  • Compression.
  • Rotation.
  • Longitudinal stretching.

Traditional stents frequently fail in this environment. Supera was specifically designed to tolerate these forces.

Ideal Indications

  • Distal SFA.
  • Popliteal artery.
  • Adductor canal lesions.
  • Mechanically stressed vessels.

Deployment Principles

Deployment technique is critical. Unlike conventional nitinol stents, Supera performance depends heavily upon implantation technique. Improper deployment may lead to:

  • Elongation.
  • Foreshortening.
  • Reduced radial force.
  • Inferior long-term patency.

Exam Pearl

Most viva examiners asking for the best stent in the popliteal artery expect the answer: "Supera Interwoven Nitinol Stent."

Clinical Evidence

Multiple studies have demonstrated excellent primary patency and exceptionally low fracture rates. Long-term outcomes remain among the best reported for femoropopliteal interventions.

Chapter 14: Covered Stents and Stent Grafts

Covered stents combine the structural properties of a stent with the sealing capability of a graft. These devices effectively create an endovascular bypass.

Construction

A covered stent consists of:

  • Metallic scaffold.
  • PTFE or similar covering.

Advantages

  • Seals perforations.
  • Excludes aneurysms.
  • Reduces plaque prolapse.
  • Provides immediate lumen restoration.

Viabahn

Viabahn is perhaps the most widely recognized self-expanding covered stent. Applications include:

  • Long SFA lesions.
  • Pseudoaneurysms.
  • Traumatic injuries.
  • Popliteal aneurysms.

VBX

VBX is a balloon-expandable covered stent. Advantages include:

  • Precise deployment.
  • High radial force.
  • Excellent ostial accuracy.

Emergency Indications

  • Arterial rupture.
  • Perforation.
  • Arteriovenous fistula.
  • Pseudoaneurysm.

Limitations

  • Side branch occlusion.
  • Thrombosis risk.
  • Need for prolonged antiplatelet therapy.

Chapter 15: Embolic Protection Devices

Distal embolization remains one of the most feared complications of peripheral intervention. Plaque, thrombus and calcium fragments may travel distally and compromise runoff vessels. Embolic protection devices were developed to reduce this risk.

SpiderFX Filter

SpiderFX is the most commonly used distal protection system in peripheral intervention. The device consists of:

  • Delivery wire.
  • Nitinol filter basket.
  • Retrieval catheter.

Mechanism

The filter is deployed distal to the lesion. During intervention, debris is captured within the basket. At the end of the procedure the filter is retrieved along with trapped embolic material.

When Should Protection Be Used?

  • Atherectomy.
  • Degenerated vein grafts.
  • Large thrombus burden.
  • Heavily calcified lesions.

Limitations

  • Cannot capture microscopic particles.
  • Requires adequate landing zone.
  • May become overloaded with debris.

Future Trends

The future likely involves integration of embolic protection with vessel preparation strategies. As atherectomy and calcium modification continue to evolve, embolic protection may become increasingly important in complex peripheral interventions.

Atherectomy without considering embolic protection is one of the most common mistakes made by beginners.

Chapter 16: Ancillary Devices – The Unsung Heroes of Peripheral Intervention

While guidewires, balloons and stents receive most of the attention, ancillary devices frequently determine procedural efficiency and safety. Experienced operators appreciate that these seemingly minor devices can transform a difficult intervention into a smooth procedure.

Torque Devices

A torque device attaches to the proximal guidewire and amplifies rotational control. Applications include:

  • Selective vessel catheterization.
  • CTO crossing.
  • Tibial interventions.
  • Microcatheter work.

The importance of precise torque transmission increases dramatically in tortuous anatomy.

Inflation Devices (Indeflators)

Inflation devices allow controlled balloon expansion. Advantages include:

  • Accurate pressure monitoring.
  • Controlled inflation.
  • Controlled deflation.
  • High-pressure capability.

Modern indeflators routinely achieve pressures exceeding 20 atmospheres.

Y Connectors

Hemostatic Y-connectors provide:

  • Hemostasis.
  • Contrast injection.
  • Guidewire manipulation.

Without a Y-connector, blood loss and air embolism risk increase significantly.

Pressure Bags

Continuous heparinized saline flushing is essential. Pressure bags maintain:

  • Catheter patency.
  • Sheath patency.
  • Thrombus prevention.

Most laboratories maintain pressure around 300 mmHg.

Wire Introducers

Wire introducers facilitate insertion of delicate guidewires through hemostatic valves. This minimizes wire damage and improves procedural efficiency.

A skilled interventionist treats ancillary devices with the same respect as guidewires and stents.

Chapter 17: Hardware Selection in Real Clinical Scenarios

Hardware selection should never be device-centered. Instead, it should be lesion-centered. The lesion determines the hardware.

Scenario 1: Focal SFA Stenosis

  • Antegrade femoral access.
  • 6F sheath.
  • Command wire.
  • PTA balloon.
  • DCB.

Most focal stenoses do not require aggressive hardware.

Scenario 2: Long SFA CTO

  • Ansel sheath.
  • Fielder FC.
  • CXI support catheter.
  • Halberd.
  • Astato XS20 if required.
  • Vessel preparation.
  • DCB ± stent.

Scenario 3: Calcified Common Femoral Artery

  • IVL.
  • Atherectomy.
  • DCB.

Historically these lesions were surgical territory. Modern calcium-modification technology has expanded endovascular options.

Scenario 4: Popliteal Lesion

  • Balloon angioplasty.
  • DCB.
  • Supera if stenting required.

Popliteal biomechanics strongly influence device choice.

Scenario 5: BTK CTO in CLTI

  • Antegrade access.
  • Progreat.
  • Fielder FC.
  • Command.
  • Gladius.
  • Retrograde pedal access if required.

BTK interventions reward patience and precision more than force.

Chapter 18: Hardware-Related Complications and Troubleshooting

Complications are inevitable. The objective is not to avoid all complications but to recognize and manage them effectively.

Guidewire Perforation

Causes:

  • Aggressive CTO wires.
  • Subintimal progression.
  • Excessive force.

Management

  • Stop advancement.
  • Confirm angiographically.
  • Prolonged balloon inflation.
  • Covered stent if required.

Balloon Rupture

Typically occurs in heavily calcified lesions. Management:

  • Careful withdrawal.
  • Repeat angiography.
  • Evaluate for dissection.

Flow-Limiting Dissection

Common after angioplasty. Management options:

  • Prolonged balloon inflation.
  • Scoring balloon.
  • Bailout stenting.

Distal Embolization

Most commonly associated with:

  • Atherectomy.
  • Thrombotic lesions.
  • Calcified lesions.

Management:

  • Aspiration thrombectomy.
  • Catheter-directed thrombolysis.
  • Mechanical thrombectomy.

Stent Underexpansion

A major cause of long-term failure. Usually reflects inadequate lesion preparation.

The best treatment for stent underexpansion is preventing it through proper vessel preparation.

Chapter 19: Major Trials and Evidence Base

Evidence-based intervention requires understanding the major studies that shaped contemporary practice.

IN.PACT SFA Trial

One of the landmark DCB studies. Demonstrated superior patency compared with conventional angioplasty. Established DCB as a major treatment modality in femoropopliteal disease.

LEVANT Trials

Evaluated Lutonix drug-coated balloons. Demonstrated improved patency and reduced repeat intervention.

ZILVER PTX Trial

One of the most important studies evaluating drug-eluting stents in peripheral arterial disease. Showed durable long-term benefit.

DEFINITIVE AR Trial

Examined directional atherectomy combined with drug-coated balloons. Highlighted the importance of vessel preparation.

SUPERA Studies

Multiple registries demonstrated:

  • High primary patency.
  • Low fracture rates.
  • Excellent performance in popliteal lesions.

Shockwave IVL Studies

Demonstrated:

  • Excellent safety profile.
  • Effective calcium modification.
  • Low dissection rates.

Viabahn Studies

Established covered stents as an important treatment option for long femoropopliteal lesions.

Modern Trend

Vessel Preparation → Drug Delivery → Implant Avoidance

This concept increasingly defines contemporary peripheral intervention.

Chapter 20: Fellowship, DM and INI-SS Examination Pearls

Common Viva Questions

Q. Why is Supera preferred in the popliteal artery?

Because its interwoven nitinol design provides superior resistance to flexion, torsion, compression and elongation.

Q. Difference between Command and Astato?

Command is a workhorse crossing wire. Astato is a dedicated penetration CTO wire.

Q. Why use a support catheter?

To improve support, torque transmission and wire exchange capability.

Q. Best treatment for severe concentric calcium?

IVL is increasingly preferred because it modifies calcium while minimizing vessel trauma.

Q. When should embolic protection be considered?

Atherectomy, thrombotic lesions, degenerated grafts and high embolic-risk interventions.

Q. Major causes of peripheral stent failure?

  • Inadequate vessel preparation.
  • Residual calcium.
  • Underexpansion.
  • Poor runoff.
  • Mechanical stress.

Q. What determines success in CTO intervention?

Support is usually more important than wire aggressiveness.

Golden Rules

  • Never escalate wires unnecessarily.
  • Always optimize support.
  • Prepare the vessel before scaffolding.
  • Avoid stenting when equivalent results can be achieved without a permanent implant.
  • Respect vessel biomechanics.

Chapter 23: Fellowship and DM-Level MCQs

MCQ 1

The primary purpose of a support catheter is:

A. Drug delivery
B. Increase support and wire control
C. Prevent restenosis
D. Vessel closure

Answer: B

MCQ 2

Which wire is generally preferred as a first-line crossing wire in a soft CTO?

A. Astato XS40
B. Winn 200T
C. Fielder FC
D. Amplatz

Answer: C

MCQ 3

The best stent for a popliteal artery lesion is:
